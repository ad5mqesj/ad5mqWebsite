I will say first that the directions supplied with the K2 are excellent - comparable to those from the Heathkits.  This is a VERY complex kit.

Definitely NOT for the faint of heart.  There are no surface mount parts, but there are a tremendous number of through hole parts, all of which must be 

placed and soldered correctly.  To make this easier the Elecraft people put all the resistors in a tape reel format in the order they are to be installed.  

This is very helpful and probably stops the biggest class of assembly errors.  However there are a LOT of capacitors, 

often looking identical but with differing values.   It is a MUST to sort them all out at the beginning and keep 

them separated in a divided box or egg cartons.   This is actually called out as a step in the manual in assembly Phase II; but its worth doing earlier.

Most of the chips are not socketed.  Only those with firmware (i.e. micro-controllers) have sockets.  On the one hand,

this makes for a more rugged device, on the other mistakes are much harder to fix.  Fortunately for me, this was not an issue.

All torroids must be wound by the builder; although there is a service available to wind the coils for you by mail order (forms included with the kit). 

Winding coils is actually not very hard, and there are excellent directions for those who have never done it before, so I would say do it yourself.

Here you can see (or not) my first real mistake.  I was careless in my inspection of the Front panel assembly before I attached the LCD 

(an essentially irreversible operation).  I failed to notice that I had not soldered pin 1 of the controller chip to the board.  

The subsequent resistance checks in the manual made this  error apparent, but t was hard to correct after the fact.  

When the manual recommends a visual inspection - DON'T SHIRK or you may live to regret it.

I was able to correct my mistake from the bottom at the expense of slightly melting the corner of the socket - Whew.

Assembly of the S.S.B. adapter at this time is actually out of sequence.  If you are not an experienced circuit builder don't do it.  

The directions here are much less verbose; they assume you have already built the K2 and are familiar 

with all the basics such as torroid winding; among other things.

Here are the photos of the end of Phase I of the assembly, ready for checkout - this is at about 11:00 on the second day.  

Here we see the next glaring mistake.  The instructions called for relays K1-17 to be installed as one the first things on the RF board; 

I mis-read it and installed K1-7.  This became apparent when the unit failed one of the tests.  On examining the schematic I realized it failed to 

actuate some of the relays because they were not installed.  Installing them at this point was significantly harder than 

it would have been earlier (at the proper time) but not too bad.  After that was corrected all tests passed.  Now we 

move on to Phase II assembly of the synthesizer and receiver.  In most respects this is the hardest phase - 

there are more components than before, jammed into a smaller space.  Personally, I think a few judicious surface mount components here 

would have made things a bit easier. Below are a couple pictures showing how I sorted out the parts into little plastic bins.

There were so many of 2 particularly common values that they came in their own bags. - 0.01µ and 0.001µF caps.

At the end of assembly I had 4 anomalies with capacitors - 1 I am sure is a factory mistake - the others could be my mistakes.

I was short two 0.01µ caps, 1 220 pF cap and one 82 pF cap.  I had leftover 1. 0.1µF, 1. 0.047µF, 1. 270 pF, and 1. 1800 pF cap.

I am quite sure that I didn't put an 82pF cap where an 1800 was called for so I think this was a factory mistake.  The others are harder to judge.  

The 0.1, 0.047, 0.01, and 0.001 caps all look very similar, many are too close to other components to see the values; its very likely that I 

just screwed up. Its probably worth while to carefully re-check the values of each group of capacitors after you place them and before soldering 

to avoid this sort of thing.  In any case I made up the short falls out of bench stock and everything seems OK.   Speaking of discrepancies: 3 insulating 

pads for TO-220 power transistors are needed and my kit contained none - I checked through the packing materials very carefully and found nothing.  

Possibly, being self adhesive, they are stuck to something I missed; in any case I had to supply my own insulating pads.

At the end of day 2 we are just a couple hours away from beginning the last phase of construction.  Here is where I began to 

have some trouble with passing tests.  I could not, initially, get the AGC control voltage into the recommended range  

This seemed non-critical so I got close and proceeded anyway. Next was the PLL Reference Oscillator 

 it is supposed to be between 9.8 and 15Khz from High to low - but I could only get it to 9.36 KHz.  Again I ignored this hoping it was 

not significant.  This may be a capacitor error.  Next is the VCO alignment - on every band; from Highest to lowest frequency, the VCO , 

must be within the control range of the synthesizer (nominal control voltages between 1.5 and 7.5).

After much fiddling, I concluded that I had most likely left off 1 turn on T5's primary.  I bunched the turns up and this mostly solved the problem. 

Now everything is nominal except the very top of 40 m (at 7300 KHz control V is 7.9) and the very bottom 

of 80m (at 3500 KHz control is 1.03).  these are off nominal (1.5 - 7.5) but some margin remains so I accepted them.

Finally we can begin the last phase of construction - the transmitter.  Here are the photos upon completion about 19:00 on the 25th.

Final alignment of the transmitter went off without a hitch - RF comes out at the advertised levels - except on 80 m - here I can only get about 2 watts, 

then the transmitter starts drawing excessive current and limits the power.

Today (day 4) I plan to re-examine the marginal PLL reference oscillator, the VCO control voltage on 40m and 80 m (in particular checking to see if 

the transmitted freq. is as advertised),  and examine the PA to determine why there is a problem on 80m.  Once I have 

completed these tasks, I will install the S.S.B. module and try it out on the air before completing the two remaining big kits (the PA and A.T.U.).

Updates will follow as progress lurches forward.  To be continued.....

 

Unsurprisingly all of the mistakes were mine.  The missing insulator pads were in with the serial number sticker.

D'Oh!  I was sure I'd seen them at some point but couldn't find them when the time came to use them.

Despite my certainty, I did indeed install an 82pf cap (marked 820) where an 1800pf cap (marked 182) was called for.

This was in the 80m low pass filter - hence the excessive current drain at 2W output.

I also found the 220pf for 270pf swap in the VCO - hence the off nominal control voltages on 40 and 80 m.  Here, however, simply

 installing the correct part did not solve the problem.  Indeed it got worse.  I am now certain that T5 has 1 extra winding.  

Here is a good thing to remember ( I clearly didn't!) when the end of the wire is poked through the torroid, as you

begin winding it, that counts as the first turn.   The next pass through the center (making 1 wrap) is actually turn 2.

I changed C72 (the one I installed wrong to begin with) back to 220pf and added 22pf in parallel and spread the turns of T5 back out.

Now all is well.  All bands VCO control voltages are well within the nominal range.  Just for fun I also tracked down

the two places where I mistakenly installed 0.01µF parts where other values were called for.  One of these

was a filter for an option connector for an option I don't have so I ignored it.  The other was filtering a supply

lead to a chip in the VCO.  I added the correct 0.1µF part on the bottom to the existing 0.01µF part I installed by mistake.

It's an excellent testament to the robustness of the design that it tolerated my errors and still worked acceptably.

After correcting these errors, I re-aligned everything and installed the KSB2 SSB adapter.

 

 

You can see it on the front right of the RF board (from the front panel's perspective).  All alignment steps were completed without 

trouble and its now ready to go on the air!

Finally here are a couple photos of the Antenna Tuner.  It was very straight forward,

took about 1/2 day to assemble and calibrate.  Finished by early evening on the 29th.

 

 

ATU Circuit boards mated on the left and in the case on the right.

This unit works great!  Press the tune button : a flurry of clicks and S.W.R. drops to 1.0:1 on every band within a second or two.  

Since the ATU remembers the settings it last used on each band, you only have to tune once for a given antenna.
